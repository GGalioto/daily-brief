🎓 Section 2

BUSINESS SCHOOL — Module 2: Revenue vs. Profit vs. Cash Flow

Curriculum module 2 of the Foundations arc. The three numbers founders mix up — and why a "profitable" business can still go bankrupt.

Here's the confusion almost every first-time founder walks into: they think "money coming in" is one number. It's actually three different numbers, measuring three different things, and mixing them up is one of the most common ways a business that looks healthy on paper actually runs out of cash.

Revenue — the total amount of money a sale generates, before you subtract anything. If Mark pays you $300/month for the GHL retainer, your revenue from Mark is $300/month. Full stop, no subtraction yet.

Profit — what's left after you subtract everything it cost you to generate that revenue. Take the Snapshot: you plan to sell it for $997. That's revenue. But subtract Gumroad or Stripe's cut (roughly 3–9% depending on platform), the GHL account cost you're already paying to build and test it, and any support hours you spend helping a buyer install it — and the number left over is profit. Revenue tells you what you made. Profit tells you what you kept.

Cash flow — the actual timing of money moving in and out of your bank account, which can be completely disconnected from both of the above. This is the one that kills businesses that are "profitable" on paper. Concrete example from your own numbers: Namecheap billed your Website Landlord hosting annually ($34.88 due July 1) — that's cash going out in one lump, regardless of whether the site has generated a single dollar of revenue yet. Flip it around: if you sign three Snapshot clients this month but two of them are on a 30-day invoice term, your revenue for the month looks great, but the cash isn't in your account yet — and if a bill comes due before that cash arrives, a "profitable" month can still leave you short.

Today's proof, from today's research feed: Profit AI — a Shopify app built by a non-technical founder using Cursor and Claude Code (the same category of tool you use daily) — exists specifically because e-commerce store owners could see their revenue in Shopify's dashboard but had no idea what their actual profit was after ad spend, cost of goods, and platform fees. That confusion is common enough that solving it is now a $30,000/month product. The lesson repeats itself everywhere: knowing your revenue and not your profit isn't a minor gap, it's a business model waiting to be sold to you.

Applying it to the AI Employee resale from Section 1: if you charge Mark $250/month for the AI Employee bundle, that's revenue. Subtract your $97/month GHL cost and any time you spend configuring it, and what's left is profit — probably $140–150/month, not $250. Cash flow is the timing question on top: GHL bills you monthly, so as long as you invoice Mark on the same cycle (not net-30), the cash flow risk here is low. That's exactly the kind of quick mental check — revenue, then profit, then cash timing — you should run before quoting any number to anyone.
