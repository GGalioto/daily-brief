2 · 🎓 Business School — Module 16

The "first 10 customers" problem — why it's a completely different game from customer 100

Here's the thing that trips up almost every first-time founder, and it's a category error rather than a skill gap: getting your first 10 customers and getting your hundredth customer are not the same activity done at different volumes. They are different jobs. People fail at the first 10 because they try to do them using the methods that work for customer 100.

Why they're different jobs

Customer 100 arrives through a system. Somebody searches a phrase, lands on a page you wrote eight months ago, reads a review from customer 40, and buys — without ever speaking to you. That's distribution compounding. Every previous customer, every article, every review makes the next sale slightly cheaper. This is the machine you're eventually building.

Customer 1 has none of that. There are no reviews, no search rankings, no referrals, no proof. There is only you, a thing you made, and a stranger with no reason to believe you. So the first 10 are not acquired by a system. They are acquired one at a time, by hand, in a way that does not scale — and that's not a failure of method, it's the correct method.

The famous framing here is Paul Graham's "do things that don't scale." The point people miss is the word don't. Manually finding, talking to, and onboarding ten people one by one is not a shortcut you're taking until the real system is ready. It's the only thing that produces what the real system needs: proof that a human being will hand you money for this.

What the first 10 actually buy you (it isn't the revenue)

Ten customers at $50 is $500. That's not the payoff. The payoff is three things you cannot get any other way:

  1. Proof of Willingness To Pay (WTP). Ten people saying "cool idea" is worth zero. One person entering a credit card is worth more than a hundred compliments. Money is the only honest signal, which is why the standard advice is to charge from customer 1 — free users tell you nothing about whether you have a business.
  2. The vocabulary. Your first ten customers tell you what they call the problem. That language becomes your landing page, your listing title, your search terms. You cannot invent it; you have to hear it.
  3. The raw material for the machine. Reviews, testimonials, case studies, before-and-after numbers, and the "who else uses this?" answer. Customer 100 arrives because customers 1 through 10 left evidence behind.

The standard 2026 playbook — and the part of it you can't use

A widely-circulated version of the solo-founder playbook (Securie, April 2026) runs five steps: (1) pick ONE specific persona — not "restaurant owners," something narrower; (2) find 100 of them by hand; (3) contact each one personally with a question about their problem, not a pitch; (4) ship to the ~10 who respond, and charge them; (5) iterate on what those 10 say, then find 10 more. The same source makes a sobering claim worth knowing: this remains the motion until roughly customer 100, and organic distribution typically doesn't take over for 12 to 18 months.

Step 3 is closed to you. One-to-one contact with strangers is a hard constraint, not a preference, and I'm not going to route around it. But look carefully at what step 3 is actually for — it is a mechanism for having ten specific conversations with ten specific people who have the problem. Direct message is one delivery method for that mechanism. It is not the mechanism.

Source: Securie — How to find your first 10 paying customers as a solo founder (April 28, 2026)

The version that works inside your constraints

Three substitutions preserve the mechanism and drop the cold outreach entirely:

  • Broadcast instead of direct message. A post in a place where the persona already gathers inverts the direction — they contact you. Slower to start, and it produces the same ten conversations. Everything in the CashMoney sell kit was built this way on purpose.
  • Warm introduction instead of cold contact. Every live prospect you have arrived through Christy or Terri. That's not luck, it's your actual working channel — and per Module 14, referral is the cheapest channel in the entire dataset, not a consolation prize.
  • Publish the question instead of asking it privately. If you want to know what independent restaurant owners hate about invoice paperwork, writing publicly about the problem attracts the people who have it and pre-qualifies them.
