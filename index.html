2🎓 Business School — Module 6

Lifetime Value (LTV) and the LTV:Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC) 3:1 rule

What it is, in plain terms: Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC), which you learned last time, is what it costs you to win one customer. Lifetime Value (LTV) is the flip side — it's the total profit (not revenue — profit, the money left after costs) you expect to collect from that one customer over the entire time they stay with you, not just their first purchase. Compare the two as a ratio and you get the single most important sentence in a subscription business: "for every dollar I spend winning a customer, how many dollars of profit do I get back over their whole relationship with me?"

Why the classic bar is 3:1, not 1:1: At exactly 1:1, you spend everything that customer will ever be worth just to acquire them — you never make a cent of profit off them, ever, even if they stay for years. Below 1:1, you're paying to lose money on every customer, forever. 3:1 is the classic floor because you need margin left over for three things beyond the CAC itself: your other fixed costs that CAC math doesn't count (your own unpaid hours, tools, overhead), a cushion for the fact that real-world churn is always a little worse than your spreadsheet assumes, and actual cash left to reinvest in growing instead of just breaking even. Below 3:1 you're not necessarily dead, but you have zero room for anything going wrong.

Worked example — Mark's HVAC retainer: From Module 3, your reseller math on Mark is $97 cost / $300 price = $203/month contribution margin. From Module 5, your CAC ranges from $150 (great case — 6 hours of your own outreach split across 2 sales) to $700 (bad case — one sale plus $400 in ads). Nobody has real churn data yet for a retainer like this, so — flagged honestly as an assumption, not measured data — let's use 12 months as a placeholder average relationship length (reasonable for a tool a contractor has to re-configure to leave, which raises switching costs and stickiness).

LTV = $203 × 12 months = $2,436.

  • Good case: $2,436 LTV ÷ $150 CAC = 16.2 : 1. Wildly above the 3:1 bar — arguably a signal you could spend more on acquisition and still come out ahead.
  • Bad case: $2,436 LTV ÷ $700 CAC = 3.5 : 1. Still technically clears the bar, but thin — and that's before counting your own uncounted time, or churn landing earlier than 12 months.

One wrinkle worth understanding now, because it previews Module 17: the Snapshot is a one-time $497–$997 sale, not a subscription. There's no "lifetime" to compound — the entire LTV of a Snapshot buyer is just that one sale's margin (roughly 87–90%, per Module 3's platform-cut math), full stop. The 3:1 rule matters enormously for Mark's retainer, where you collect margin every month for as long as he stays. It matters far less for the Snapshot, where you get one shot at margin per customer and then need a brand-new customer to make another dollar. That's the real difference between a product business and a services/subscription business — and why the two lanes you're running side by side behave completely differently even though they share a price tag.
