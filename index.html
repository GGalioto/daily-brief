🎓 Business School — Fixed vs. Variable Costs

Every cost in a business falls into one of two buckets. Fixed costs show up whether you do one sale or a thousand — rent, a software subscription, insurance. Variable costs move with volume — the per-unit material, the per-minute phone bill, the payment processing fee on each transaction. Most real businesses are a blend, but which one dominates changes everything about how you should price and how fast you can grow.

Here's the rule that actually matters: if your costs are mostly fixed, every additional sale after you cover the fixed nut is close to pure profit. If your costs are mostly variable, growth never gets cheaper — you need volume just to stay even, and every unit has to individually cover its own cost or you lose money faster the more you sell.

Worked example 1 — Mark's HVAC retainer (mixed cost structure)

The GoHighLevel AI Employee subscription Mark's account runs on is $97/month flat — fixed, whether he gets 5 calls or 500 that month. But the Voice AI usage is metered at roughly $0.06/minute — variable. A typical HVAC operation logging 200–300 minutes of after-hours and overflow calls a month lands around $120–$180/month all-in once you stack the metered piece on the flat subscription (ACHR News, "Missed Calls, Lost Jobs," June 7). Double the call volume and the variable piece roughly doubles — the $97 doesn't move. This is why Geo can't price Mark's retainer to just cover the $97: if he prices it too tight and Mark's call volume spikes in a busy month, the variable minutes quietly eat the margin. A "great month for Mark" could turn into a break-even month for Geo if the retainer doesn't have room to flex.

Worked example 2 — the GHL HVAC Snapshot (mostly-fixed cost structure)

Geo's hours building the three automations (calendar → Job Booked, the 5-step nurture, the review-request gate) are already spent — a sunk, fixed cost that doesn't change whether he sells one copy or a hundred. The only cost that scales with each additional sale is the platform's cut — roughly 10% on Gumroad plus card processing (~2.9% + $0.30), or whatever the GHL Marketplace takes on its own listings (insightraider.com; gohighlevelgrowthstack.com) — genuinely variable, but tiny relative to a $497–$997 price tag. That's the entire reason a packaged digital product scales in a way a retainer never can: Mark's retainer costs Geo his own time on every additional client (a variable cost that eventually needs hiring to scale past), while the Snapshot's marginal cost per extra buyer is nearly zero. Selling the 50th copy costs almost the same as selling the 5th. That leverage gap — services vs. products — is exactly what Module 17 later in this curriculum will dig into directly.
