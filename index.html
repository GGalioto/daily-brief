2 · 🎓 BUSINESS SCHOOL — MODULE 9

Recurring revenue math: Monthly Recurring Revenue (MRR), churn, and why churn kills more startups than slow growth

MRR is your subscription revenue, normalized to a month. Mark's future $300/month retainer = $300 of MRR. Sell the GoHighLevel (GHL) Snapshot for $497 one time and that's real revenue — but it is not MRR, because nothing repeats. The whole reason investors and buyers obsess over MRR is predictability: $300 of MRR is worth more than a $300 one-time sale because it's also next month's $300, and the month after, without re-earning it.

Churn is the percentage of that recurring revenue (or those customers) that leaves each period. Two flavors: logo churn (what % of customers cancel) and revenue churn (what % of dollars cancel — they differ when customers pay different amounts). At your scale, treat them as the same thing.

The leaky bucket — the one formula worth memorizing

Growth is water pouring into a bucket; churn is a hole in the bottom. The bucket does not fill forever. Your MRR mathematically stops growing at the point where new revenue added per month equals revenue lost per month:

MRR ceiling = new MRR added per month ÷ monthly churn rate

1 new client/mo × $300, at 5% monthly churn:
$300 ÷ 0.05 = $6,000 MRR ceiling (20 clients — growth stops there, forever)

Same effort, 2% monthly churn:
$300 ÷ 0.02 = $15,000 MRR ceiling (50 clients)

Read that again: the exact same sales effort produces a 2.5× bigger business purely because the hole is smaller. That's why churn kills more startups than a lack of growth — a company adding customers at a heroic pace with 8% monthly churn plateaus early and then shrinks the moment sales slow down, while a boring 2%-churn business compounds quietly. And every churned customer also torches the Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC) you paid for them — if they leave before the payback period you learned in Module 8, that customer was a straight loss.

What "normal" looks like — real 2026 benchmarks

For Software as a Service (SaaS) sold to Small and Medium-sized Businesses (SMBs) — exactly your customer type — Optifai's benchmark across 939 companies puts healthy monthly churn at 3–5%, with UserJot calling 2–4% "healthy" and under 3% "solid". That sounds small until you compound it: 5% monthly churn = roughly 46% of your customers gone within a year. Enterprise software gets under 1% monthly — mostly because annual contracts make leaving an event instead of a default. Lesson hiding in that: contract length is a churn tool. A 12-month term on a retainer isn't just security, it mathematically caps your hole.

Applied to your actual portfolio

  • The HVAC retainer is your compounding asset. Its churn lever is being load-bearing: a system that provably books real jobs is agony to cancel; a dashboard nobody opens churns at the first budget review.
  • The GHL affiliate program's 40% recurring commission (confirmed current) is someone else's MRR passed through to you — it churns at whatever rate your referred agencies fail. Same leaky bucket, one level removed.
  • CashMoney's $50 one-time price is the deliberate opposite trade: zero churn risk, zero compounding. Fine for a validation experiment; if it ever proves demand, the five subscription competitors in Section 1 are showing you what the recurring version prices at ($19–$99/mo).

Explain-it-back test: "MRR is the water coming in, churn is the hole, and my business stops growing at exactly (monthly new MRR ÷ churn rate) — so halving churn is worth as much as doubling sales." If you can say that cold, Module 9 is done. Next up: Module 10, Positioning.
