🎓 Business School: Pricing Strategy — How to Actually Land On a Number

Every founder eventually hits the same wall: "okay, but what do I actually charge?" There are three honest ways to answer that question, and most people accidentally use the weakest one. Here's all three, applied directly to the Snapshot — the exact product Geo has been avoiding pricing for a month.

1. Cost-Plus Pricing — the floor

Add up what it cost you to build the thing, add a margin on top, done. For the Snapshot, the real build cost is mostly sunk hours (already spent, non-recoverable) plus a thin variable slice — the roughly 10-13% platform/processing cut GHL and payment processors take per sale. A pure cost-plus number would land low, maybe $150-200, because it only accounts for what it cost Geo, not what it's worth to the buyer. Cost-plus tells you the floor — the price below which you're actively losing money once you count your own time as worth something. It should never be the ceiling.

2. Value-Based Pricing — the ceiling

Price based on what the outcome is worth to the buyer, not what it cost you to build. Industry data on contractor time-savings from Artificial Intelligence (AI) automation (cited in this brief before) shows contractors saving 3+ hours a week once these systems are running — and the actual point of the Snapshot's 3 automations is recovering booked jobs that would otherwise go to voicemail. If the system recovers even one additional job a month at a typical HVAC ticket size, that single job is worth many multiples of the Snapshot's price. Value-based pricing says: charge close to what that recovered revenue is worth, not what your hours cost. A Forbes analysis on Software as a Service (SaaS) pricing strategy found companies that switch from cost-plus to value-based pricing see 5-10% higher revenue on average — the gap between the two methods is real money left on the table, every time.

3. Competitor-Anchored Pricing — the reality check

What are buyers already used to paying for something like this? Today's marketplace scan gives a real anchor range: Epic HVAC Snapshot lists $497-$2,497 (with a "Black Friday" $497 anchor against a $2,497 list price), HVAC Snapshot for GHL sits at $997. That range is the market's existing expectation — price meaningfully below it and buyers assume something's wrong with the product; price within or above it (with real positioning to back it up) and you're read as legitimate.

How to actually pick the number: use cost-plus as a sanity floor (never go below it), value-based as the honest ceiling (what it's actually worth), and competitor-anchored as the reality check on what the market already expects to pay. Blending those three for the Snapshot: floor is roughly $200, ceiling is "worth many multiples of $997 if it saves even one job," and the competitor anchor sits at $497-$2,497. The trap most solo builders fall into is racing to the bottom of the competitor range to "be competitive" — but in a crowded category, the bottom of the range reads as the cheapest, not the safest choice, and it's the fastest way to make zero margin on something genuinely worth more. Recommendation: anchor at $997 — it matches the strongest-positioned named competitor, sits well inside the value-based ceiling, and avoids signaling "budget option" in a field that's about to have six or more sellers.
