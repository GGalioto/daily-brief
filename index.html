🎓 Business School — Module 7: Margin vs. Markup

The single most common founder pricing mistake: treating "margin" and "markup" as the same number. They're not, and confusing them makes people price themselves into a loss without realizing it.

  • Markup = profit ÷ what it cost you. "I marked it up 200%" means you charged 3x your cost.
  • Margin = profit ÷ what the customer paid. It can never exceed 100% (you can't keep more than 100% of the sale price as profit). Markup, on the other hand, has no ceiling — you can mark something up 500%, 1,000%, whatever.

Worked example #1 — Mark's reseller lane: You pay GHL $97/month (your cost). You charge Mark $300/month. Profit = $203.

MetricFormulaResult
Markup$203 ÷ $97 (cost)209%
Margin$203 ÷ $300 (price)67.7%

Same $203 profit, two very different-sounding numbers. If you'd mentally treated "209% markup" as "209% margin," you'd be modeling a business that's mathematically impossible — margin tops out at 100%.

Worked example #2 — today's research handed us a live one: a competing HVAC Snapshot vendor (Epic HVAC Snapshot, on the HighLevel Marketplace) lists its snapshot at $2,497 "regular price," discounted to $497 under permanent-looking "Black Friday" branding. That's an 80% sticker discount — a markup/anchor story designed to make $497 feel like a steal. It says nothing about their actual margin, because we don't know their real cost basis. This is a sales-copy trick, not a pricing calculation: big invented "markup" from an inflated anchor price, sold as a "margin gift" to the buyer.

Why this matters for you: when you price the Snapshot task ($497–$997 is the going range per today's research — see lane verdict), don't just ask "what's my markup over my build cost?" Sunk build hours aren't a per-unit cost, so markup math on them is close to infinite and useless. Ask the margin question instead: "of the $997 sale price, what % is actually mine to keep after payment processing (~10–13% variable, per your own 07-09 numbers)?" That's roughly 87–90% margin — a genuinely excellent number, and the one that should drive your pricing confidence, not an arbitrary markup multiple.
