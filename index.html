🎓 Business School — Module 3: Unit Economics 101

The concept: every business has three numbers hiding inside a single sale: cost per unit (what it actually costs you to deliver one unit of your product/service), price per unit (what the customer pays), and contribution margin (price minus cost — what's left over to cover your fixed costs and eventually become profit). Most first-time founders only track price. That's like knowing your paycheck but not your rent.

Your worked example: Mark's GHL reseller math

You already made this decision back on 2026-06-16 without naming it — you were doing unit economics instinctively. Here's the breakdown, made explicit:

Cost per unit = what GoHighLevel charges you underneath, per client, per month. On the Reseller/SaaS lane, that's the $97/month Starter plan cost you eat so Mark doesn't have to see it.
Price per unit = what Mark pays you, all-in — roughly $300/month (software + your management retainer bundled).
Contribution margin = $300 − $97 = $203/month, per client. That $203 is what's actually yours to keep — not the $300 headline number.

Now stack today's research on top: agencies are reselling GHL's AI Employee add-on (itself a $97/month cost) at $200–$600/month to local businesses (netpartners.marketing, verified against HighLevel Support Portal, 2026). If you add that line to Mark's account: cost per unit rises by $97, price per unit could rise by $200–$500 (an "AI Receptionist Package" upsell), so contribution margin on that one add-on alone is $103–$403/month. Layer it on top of the existing $203 and one client could be worth $306–$606/month in pure contribution margin — before you've signed client #2.

This is also why "10 clients at $300/month = $3,000/month" is the wrong way to think about scale. It's 10 clients × $203 contribution margin = $2,030/month after the GHL cost — and that's before your own time (which isn't free, even if it feels that way right now). Tomorrow's module (Fixed vs. Variable Costs) picks up exactly here: is your $203 margin per client mostly eaten by fixed costs (the same regardless of client count) or variable ones (that scale with clients)? That's the number that decides whether client #11 is almost pure profit or costs you almost as much as client #1.
