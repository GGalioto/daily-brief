🎓 Business School — Module 18

Contracts & scope creep — the basic protections a solo consultant needs before scaling client work.

Scope creep is any expansion of a project's deliverables, timeline, or complexity that happens without a matching increase in what you're paid. It's not rare — large projects run 27–45% over their original cost estimate on average, and almost none of that overrun shows up as a dramatic single ask. It arrives one small "can you also just—" at a time.

The root cause is almost never a difficult client. It's an unclear contract. "We'll handle Rainbow's environmental notifications" leaves room for a dozen different interpretations. "We file and monitor Ohio National Emission Standards for Hazardous Air Pollutants (NESHAP) demolition/asbestos notifications for jobs Rainbow scopes to us, under our own delegated filing account, for the addresses and job types listed in the engagement" does not.

The fix has three parts, all decided before the first dollar changes hands: name every deliverable specifically (which state, which form type, which trigger events), name what's explicitly excluded (advising on abatement method, handling an EPA audit, filing in a state that hasn't been built yet), and set a change-order process for anything new (acknowledge the request, confirm it's outside scope, quote it separately — don't just absorb it to be helpful).

Why this lane specifically, and why now: CashMoney is structurally immune to this problem — it's a $50 one-time sale, there's no ongoing relationship to expand. The Notification & Compliance lane is the opposite: it's becoming Geo's first real recurring service relationship, built on eBiz delegation (Forge holding its own filing credential, doing ongoing work for Rainbow). That structure is exactly where scope creep lives — "since you're already filing our Ohio notifications, can you also just watch our Kentucky renewal dates" is a reasonable-sounding ask that, without a written boundary, quietly turns a scoped pilot into an unpaid ongoing commitment. The Indiana/Kentucky expansion itself is fine — it's the planned, priced version of that same ask. The unscoped version, agreed to verbally in a hallway because it felt small, is the one that costs money later.

Worked example: before Rainbow ever becomes a live filing client, the engagement should say, in writing, which state(s), which notification type(s) (original vs. revision), and which triggering events (a schedule change, a contractor change) are covered — and that anything outside that list gets a quote, not a favor.

The AI-governance gap = an opportunity that isn't "build another AI tool." Pax8's numbers above pair with Thryv's April survey (66% small-business AI adoption, 70% saying they need more training) to describe the same shape of gap from two directions: businesses are buying and using AI faster than they're writing down any rules for how it gets used, or training anyone properly on it. Zero build required, content-led distribution fits the no-cold-outreach constraint, and it's a natural extension of the consulting instincts Geo already has — a genuinely new whitespace, not a repackaged lane.
