1. Expired-Patent Product Scout for Amazon/Private-Label Sellers
PASSIVE
📊 Evidence: $38K/mo documented, Startupheist, June 29, 2026

The U.S. patent system is full of consumer inventions that expired without ever reaching a shelf. Mine public patent data continuously, filter for simple buildable consumer products, cross-reference against real Amazon demand signals, and sell curated product briefs to private-label sellers as a subscription feed, with higher-ticket custom scouting reports on top. 500 subscribers at $49/month is $24.5K; add four $3,500 bespoke reports and you clear $38K solo.

On-ramp for you: zero overlap with local service businesses or GHL. Requires learning patent search (Google Patents is free and searchable) plus basic Amazon demand-research tools (Jungle Scout-style, ~$49/month entry). Time to first paid subscriber: realistically 60-90 days once the scoring model works. Fragility check: the moat is judgment/interpretation, not data access — a well-funded competitor (Helium 10, Jungle Scout) could bolt this on as a feature if it proves out, so first-mover speed matters more than defensibility.

2. Fixed-Price AI Batch Job Shop for Agencies (Not Local Services)
SEMI-ACTIVE
📊 Evidence: $11,241 plausible early month, Startupheist, June 30, 2026 — built on Google's Colab CLI shipped June 5

Ecommerce agencies and Direct-To-Consumer (DTC) brands sit on catalog backlogs (background removal, listing cleanup, image variants) that are too small and irregular for a custom AI agency to bother with, but too repetitive to do by hand. Google's new Colab command-line interface turns borrowed cloud GPUs into a programmable batch-processing lane. Package five repeatable cleanup recipes at fixed prices, route each job to whichever execution lane is cheapest (a polished API like Photoroom at $0.02/image, or Colab for custom batch logic), and sell the finished folder, not the compute.

On-ramp for you: uses your building/automation instincts, but the buyer is an ecommerce agency or DTC brand, not a local contractor or attorney — a genuinely different customer base and sales motion than anything you're running today. Capital required: near zero (Colab CLI is free tier to start). Fragility check: price-competitive against improving raw AI APIs — the "job shop" wrapper has to keep finding new repeatable recipes as the underlying tools get better and cheaper, or the margin erodes.

3. Solo Portfolio of Narrow AI Point-Products (Not Custom Client Builds)
ACTIVE
📊 Evidence: ~$300K/mo across ~20 products, one operator, reported June 22, 2026 (Danny Postma / Postcrafts)

A fundamentally different model than everything else in your portfolio: instead of custom-building automations for individual clients (consulting, Anvil), build narrow, self-serve AI products (a headshot generator, a hairstyle-preview tool, a translation app) sold direct to a broad consumer or small-business audience at a flat subscription, with no per-customer build work after launch. The reported extreme case — one operator running ~20 such products — is not the realistic target, but the model itself (60-80% operating margins, no payroll, find a specific paid pain point and wrap existing AI APIs around it cleanly) is a genuinely different business than "build a system for one client at a time."

On-ramp for you: this uses the same AI-fluency and rapid-build instincts you're already developing through GHL and Anvil work, but points them at a self-serve product instead of a managed service — meaning revenue could eventually decouple from your hours. Time to first dollar: a narrow product can ship in days to weeks if the demand is real and the wrapper is thin. Fragility check: distribution is the hard part, not the build — dozens of thin AI wrappers launch and die weekly; the ones that work found a specific, already-searching audience first (Search Engine Optimization (SEO)-driven or existing-audience-driven), not the other way around. Don't start building before you've found the searching audience.
