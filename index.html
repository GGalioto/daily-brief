🎓 Business School: TAM / SAM / SOM — Why Big Market-Size Slides Are Mostly Theater

Every funding announcement leads with a market-size number. Avoca's says the U.S. HVAC market alone is worth $50 billion in 2025, projected to $75 billion by 2032. That number is real. It's also almost useless for deciding what you should do next — because it's measuring the wrong thing at your stage. Here's the framework that separates the number that matters from the number that just sounds impressive.

Total Addressable Market (TAM)

The total revenue available if you captured 100% of the market — every HVAC contractor in America, using every AI tool, spending every dollar they'd ever spend on it. Avoca's $50B is a TAM. It's a real number, but no single company (not Avoca, not ServiceTitan, not you) will ever capture all of it. TAM answers "how big could this industry theoretically get," not "how big could my business get." It's the number VCs want to see on slide 3, because a $50B TAM makes a $1B valuation look conservative — that's exactly why it's a sales tool, not a planning tool.

Serviceable Addressable Market (SAM)

The slice of the TAM you could actually reach with your specific product and business model. Not every HVAC contractor is a candidate for an AI front-office tool — most are too small, some don't take enough inbound calls to need it, some already have a receptionist they trust. For Avoca, SAM might be "HVAC/plumbing/electrical companies with $1M+ revenue and enough call volume to justify automation" — probably a few hundred thousand businesses, not millions. For Geo's Snapshot, SAM is even narrower: HVAC contractors who are already on GoHighLevel — the platform is a hard prerequisite. That's a small, specific, findable slice of the $50B TAM.

Serviceable Obtainable Market (SOM)

What you can realistically capture in the near term, given your actual resources, distribution, and competition. This is the number that should drive decisions. Avoca's disclosed SOM is 800 customers — out of a TAM measured in the hundreds of thousands of businesses. For the Snapshot, a realistic year-one SOM might be 10–50 sales through the GHL Marketplace and Gumroad, working solo with zero sales team. That's the number to build a plan around — not $50 billion.

Why this matters for the Snapshot specifically: a $50B TAM slide would be true and also completely irrelevant to whether packaging the listing this week is worth 3 hours of Geo's time. The real question is SOM: is there a reachable slice of GHL-using HVAC contractors big enough to hit even 10 sales at $497–$997? Given roughly 800+ agencies already selling HVAC-adjacent GHL snapshots (per the Epic HVAC Snapshot and HVAC Snapshot for GHL listings found in prior briefs) and Avoca's proof that contractors are actively paying for this category, the answer looks like yes — but "yes" was never going to come from the TAM number.
