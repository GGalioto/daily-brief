🎓 Business School: Moats (and Which Ones You Actually Have)

Last module (Positioning) set the table: position = "who it's for, what it replaces, why you." This module is the follow-up: what keeps competitors out once you've won? That's a moat.

There are 5 classic types:

1. Network Effects

Value increases with every user who joins (Facebook, Slack). Your product is worthless with one user, invaluable with a million. Available to you as a GHL consultant? No. GHL itself has network effects (more agencies using it = more ecosystem), but you don't.

2. Switching Costs

Expensive or painful to leave once in (enterprise software, medical records, deeply integrated automations). If you build a system Mark depends on daily—call-to-invoice pipeline that works—he faces real cost to replace it (time, risk of downtime). Moderate moat for you. But it's fragile: if he leaves GHL, your moat evaporates. The platform owns the stronger moat.

3. Brand

Customers choose you over alternatives because of your name/reputation (Apple, Coca-Cola, your name as "the HVAC GHL consultant"). This is weak-to-moderate for you today but can be built. Takes time—reputation compounds slowly—and it's fragile. One bad install erodes years of brand work.

4. Cost Advantage

You can profitably sell at lower prices than competitors (Costco's bulk scale, Walmart's supply chain). You don't have this as a consultant. You sell services (time-bounded), not products with scaling economies. Your marginal cost per unit is close to your price.

5. Regulatory

Legal barriers to entry (licenses, patents, compliance requirements). Not available to you. GHL handles the compliance; you ride their moat, not build your own.

What this means: As a GHL consultant, your only real moat is (a) being the recognized expert for [vertical] + [problem], and (b) building switching costs through deep, tailored automation work. That's why positioning (Module 10) comes before moats. Your moat IS your differentiation.

Worked example — The Snapshot: A generic "5-step follow-up template" has zero moat (copy-paste commodity). But "purpose-built for HVAC missed-call-to-invoice flow, written by someone who understands contractor seasonality + objection handling" has moat (positioning + trust). That positioning is what lets you charge $497 instead of $97. That positioning is your defense against commoditization.
