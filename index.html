2. 🎓 BUSINESS SCHOOL — Payback Period

You've learned Revenue vs. Profit vs. Cash Flow. You know Unit Economics (cost + price + margin). You know Fixed vs. Variable Costs. Now the practical question: when does a customer actually pay for themselves?

The Concept

Payback period = how many months of profit from one customer it takes to recover the money you spent to acquire that customer.

Formula: Payback period (months) = CAC ÷ (contribution margin per month)

Why It Matters

A customer who pays for themselves in 3 weeks is fundamentally different from a customer who takes 12 months. The fast-payback customer gives you real cash flow (money coming in to fund the next hire, ad spend, or product build). The slow-payback customer looks profitable on paper but might drain you before the profit actually arrives. This is why "profitable" and "viable" are not the same thing.

Your HVAC Example

Let's use Mark's deal:

  • CAC (Customer Acquisition Cost): You spent 6 hours getting Mark to sign, at your effective rate of $50/hour = $300. (Actually achieved: you landed him in 2 hours, so real CAC = $100, but let's be conservative.)
  • Contribution margin per month: Mark pays $300/month for the GHL setup, you pay $97 for his GHL license, leaving $203 profit per month.
  • Payback period: $300 CAC ÷ $203/month = 1.48 months = roughly 6 weeks. (At the real $100 CAC: 3 weeks.)

Translation: Mark pays for himself in less than a month. After that, every month is profit that funds the next customer acquisition or product build.

Contrast: Paid Ads

If instead of your time, you spent $400 on ads to land a customer (CAC = $700 total):

  • Payback period: $700 ÷ $203 = 3.4 months

Still reasonable (under 4 months is typical), but you're bleeding cash for over 3 months before that customer becomes profitable. And if your churn happens in month 4, you break even or lose money.

The Hidden Lesson

Payback period is why organic growth (your time, content, community) scales differently from paid growth (ads, outsourced sales). With organic CAC, you can afford to wait 3-6 months for payback because you're not burning cash the whole time — you're working. With paid CAC, a short payback period is non-negotiable, because cash leaves your account on day 1.

This is the real reason the Snapshot ($497 one-time sale, ~$150 CAC organic, ~6 hours build) makes sense at your scale. One sale pays for itself in pure revenue. A $300 consulting retainer pays for itself in a month. But a $5K-per-month AI tool with a $2K CAC doesn't work unless you can fund 2-3 months of cash burn while customers accumulate.
